Life and Mind: Did Consciousness Precede Life or Emerge From It?

Chapter 4 Part IV: Consciousness-First Hypotheses

4.1 Chapter 7: Panpsychism and the Primacy of Experience

4.1.1 7.1 The Panpsychist Thesis

Panpsychism is the view that consciousness (or at least proto-consciousness) is a fundamental and ubiquitous feature of reality. On this view, consciousness does not emerge at a certain level of complexity but is present “all the way down.”

Historical roots: Panpsychism has ancient antecedents (e.g., pre-Socratic philosophy, Vedantic thought) and was entertained by modern philosophers including Spinoza, Leibniz, William James, and Alfred North Whitehead.

Contemporary forms:

  • Micropsychism: Elementary particles or fields possess minimal experiential properties.
  • Cosmopsychism: The universe as a whole is conscious, and individual consciousnesses are derivative.
  • Priority monism + panpsychism: Fundamental reality is a conscious whole; parts inherit consciousness.

4.1.2 7.2 Arguments for Panpsychism

1. Avoiding the hard problem: If consciousness is fundamental, it need not be explained in terms of non-conscious elements. The hard problem dissolves.

2. Russellian monism: Physics describes only the extrinsic, relational properties of matter. The intrinsic nature of matter may be experiential. Russell noted that we know matter’s structure but not its intrinsic character—which we do know in the case of our own minds. Perhaps the intrinsic nature of all matter is proto-experiential.

3. Continuity in nature: Panpsychism provides a continuous story from simple to complex consciousness, avoiding arbitrary thresholds.

4. No emergence ex nihilo: If non-conscious matter cannot produce consciousness, consciousness must have been present from the start.

4.1.3 7.3 The Combination Problem

The combination problem is the central challenge for panpsychism: How do micro-level experiences (e.g., in particles or cells) combine to form unified macro-level experiences (e.g., human consciousness)?

Sub-problems:

  • The structural mismatch problem: My experience has a unified phenomenal character; my brain has billions of neurons. How do billions of micro-experiences combine into one?
  • The boundary problem: What determines the boundaries of a conscious subject? Why am I one subject, not a billion?
  • The incompatible quality problem: If micro-experiences have qualities (e.g., “redness”), how do these combine without incoherence?

Panpsychists have proposed various solutions (emergent subjects, priority monism, Hegelian fusion), but the problem remains contested.

4.1.4 7.4 Panpsychism and the Origin of Life

If panpsychism is true, then proto-conscious properties existed before life. Life would not create consciousness but organize pre-existing proto-conscious elements into increasingly unified and complex forms.

Implications:

  • The origin of life is the origin of organized, autonomous proto-consciousness.
  • FUCA and early cells did not generate sentience but structured it.
  • Evolution is not just the evolution of form but the evolution of experience. \[frontiersin.org\]

This aligns with recent proposals that cells are conscious. If panpsychism is true, cellular consciousness is not surprising—it is a natural step in the organization of proto-conscious matter. \[frontiersin.org\]

4.1.5 Summary

Panpsychism proposes that consciousness is fundamental and ubiquitous. It avoids the hard problem by making consciousness a basic feature of reality, but faces the combination problem. If panpsychism is correct, the origin of life involved the organization of proto-conscious elements into autopoietic, autonomous systems, marking a transition in the structure of experience rather than its emergence from nothing.

4.1.6 Open Question

If panpsychism is true and proto-consciousness exists at the fundamental level, what distinguishes the rich, unified experience of a human from the (presumably) minimal, dispersed proto-experiences of particles?

4.2 Chapter 8: Information, Meaning, and Proto-Consciousness

4.2.1 8.1 Information as a Bridge Concept

Information has emerged as a central concept in both physics and biology, potentially bridging the gap between matter and mind.

Shannon information (classical information theory) measures uncertainty reduction and is substrate-neutral: the same information can be encoded in bits, neurons, or DNA.

Semantic information involves meaning and reference, not just statistical correlation. This is the kind of information relevant to cognition and consciousness.

Integrated information (IIT) proposes that consciousness is identical to a system’s capacity to integrate information irreducibly.

4.2.2 8.2 Biosemiotics: Life as Sign-Interpretation

Biosemiotics applies semiotic theory (the study of signs and meaning) to biology. Jesper Hoffmeyer and Marcello Barbieri argue that life is fundamentally based on semiosis—the production and interpretation of signs.

Examples:

  • The genetic code is a semiotic system: DNA sequences (signs) are interpreted by ribosomes to produce proteins (meanings).
  • Cellular signaling involves sign-interpretation: hormones (signs) bind to receptors, triggering cascades (meanings).
  • Ecological interactions involve sign-use: a predator interprets tracks (signs) as indicating prey (meanings).

Implications:

  • Meaning-making is not confined to minds but is intrinsic to life.
  • Semiosis may require proto-subjective interpretation, linking meaning to proto-consciousness.

4.2.3 8.3 Does Information Processing Require Consciousness?

A key question: Can information be meaningful without a subjective perspective to interpret it?

Functionalist answer: No. Meaning is determined by functional role (causal connections), independent of consciousness.

Phenomenological answer: Yes. Meaning requires a “for-whom”—a subject for whom the sign has significance.

Enactivists argue that meaning arises through sense-making: an autonomous agent distinguishes relevant from irrelevant based on its norms (survival, goals). Sense-making may entail minimal sentience.

4.2.4 8.4 Proto-Consciousness in Prebiotic Systems?

If information processing or semiosis entails proto-consciousness, could prebiotic chemical systems already possess it?

Speculative scenario: Self-organizing chemical networks (e.g., autocatalytic sets, RNA replicators) exhibit rudimentary autonomy and responsiveness. If autonomy entails minimal sentience (as enactivists suggest), then proto-consciousness may precede cellular life. \[frontiersin.org\]

Objections:

  • This conflates functional responsiveness with subjective experience.
  • Autonomy may be necessary but not sufficient for consciousness.

Counter-reply:

  • If we accept panpsychism or enactivism, the boundary between responsiveness and sentience becomes porous.

4.2.5 Summary

Information and meaning-making are central to life. Biosemiotics suggests that semiosis (sign-interpretation) is fundamental to biology. If meaning requires subjectivity, and life involves meaning-making, then life may inherently involve proto-consciousness. This opens the possibility that consciousness-like properties existed even in prebiotic chemical systems, though this remains deeply controversial.

4.2.6 Open Question

If a self-replicating RNA molecule distinguishes “correct” from “incorrect” copying, does this constitute a minimal form of meaning-making—and if so, does it entail proto-consciousness?
