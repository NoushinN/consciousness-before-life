Life and Mind: Did Consciousness Precede Life or Emerge From It?

Chapter 3 Part III: The Science of Consciousness

3.1 Chapter 5: Neural Correlates of Consciousness

3.1.1 5.1 The Search for the NCC

The neural correlates of consciousness (NCC) are the minimal neural mechanisms jointly sufficient for a specific conscious experience. Identifying the NCC has been a central goal of consciousness neuroscience.

Pioneering work by Christof Koch, Francis Crick, and others sought to pinpoint brain regions and processes that differentiate conscious from unconscious states. Early hypotheses focused on:

  • The thalamocortical system: Thalamus and cortex interact in loops that may sustain consciousness.
  • 40 Hz oscillations: Gamma-band neural synchrony was proposed as a “binding” mechanism integrating distributed information.
  • Re-entrant processing: Feedback connections allow higher cortical areas to modulate lower areas, creating dynamic, recursive processing.

3.1.2 5.2 Landmark 2025 Experiment: IIT vs. GNWT

A landmark 2025 study directly tested two leading theories: Integrated Information Theory (IIT) and Global Neuronal Workspace Theory (GNWT). \[sciencedaily.com\]

  • IIT (Tononi, Koch) proposes that consciousness arises from integrated information (Φ). A system is conscious to the extent that it integrates information in an irreducible way. \[sciencedaily.com\]
  • GNWT (Baars, Dehaene) proposes that consciousness arises when information is broadcast widely across the brain through a “global workspace,” making it available to multiple cognitive systems. \[sciencedaily.com\]

The 2025 adversarial collaboration involved 256 subjects and used brain imaging (fMRI, EEG, MEG) to track neural activity during conscious and unconscious visual perception. \[sciencedaily.com\]

Key findings:

  • Neither theory was decisively confirmed or refuted. \[sciencedaily.com\]
  • Consciousness correlated more strongly with early sensory processing (posterior visual cortex) than with prefrontal executive areas. \[sciencedaily.com\]
  • The prefrontal cortex, emphasized by GNWT, was less critical for consciousness than expected. \[sciencedaily.com\]
  • IIT’s prediction of sustained posterior integration was not robustly supported. \[sciencedaily.com\]

Implications: Consciousness may be more tightly linked to perception than to reasoning—“consciousness is about being, not doing”. This challenges theories that locate consciousness in higher-order cognitive processes. \[sciencedaily.com\]

3.1.3 5.3 Access vs. Phenomenal Consciousness

A longstanding debate concerns the relationship between access consciousness (A-consciousness) and phenomenal consciousness (P-consciousness).

  • A-consciousness: Information is accessible for reasoning, report, and behavioral control.
  • P-consciousness: Subjective experience, qualia, “what it is like.”

Ned Block argues these are dissociable: one can have phenomenal experience without access (e.g., fleeting sensations not reportable) and access without phenomenal feel (e.g., unconscious priming). \[en.wikipedia.org\]

Others, like Daniel Dennett, argue that the distinction is illusory: all consciousness is access consciousness, and phenomenal feel is a cognitive construction. \[en.wikipedia.org\]

The 2025 findings suggest that perception—rich in phenomenal character—may be more central to consciousness than access. \[sciencedaily.com\]

3.1.4 5.4 The Hard Problem Revisited: Explanatory Gaps and Philosophical Zombies

Even if we identify the NCC, the hard problem remains: why are these neural processes accompanied by experience? \[en.wikipedia.org\]

Philosophical zombies—beings physically identical to us but lacking consciousness—are often invoked to illustrate the hard problem. If zombies are conceivable, this suggests consciousness is not logically entailed by physical facts. Critics argue zombies are not genuinely conceivable or that conceivability does not imply possibility. \[en.wikipedia.org\]

The explanatory gap (Levine) highlights that even complete neural descriptions leave unexplained why particular experiences feel the way they do. Why does C-fiber firing feel like pain rather than something else—or nothing at all? \[en.wikipedia.org\]

3.1.5 Summary

Neuroscience has made significant progress in identifying neural correlates of consciousness, with recent findings emphasizing sensory perception over executive control. However, identifying correlates does not solve the hard problem of why these processes are accompanied by experience. The explanatory gap remains a central philosophical challenge.

3.1.6 Open Question

If consciousness is more tightly linked to sensory perception than to reasoning, does this suggest that simpler organisms with basic sensory systems—such as insects or even single cells—are also conscious?

3.2 Chapter 6: Theories of Consciousness: A Comparative Overview

3.2.1 6.1 Integrated Information Theory (IIT)

Integrated Information Theory, developed by Giulio Tononi and colleagues, is one of the most ambitious theories of consciousness. \[sciencedaily.com\]

Core claims:

  • Consciousness is identical to integrated information (Φ, “phi”).
  • A system is conscious to the degree that it integrates information irreducibly—information that cannot be reduced to independent parts.
  • Consciousness is intrinsic (exists from the system’s own perspective), structured (has specific phenomenal content), definite (has precise boundaries), and unified.

Implications:

  • IIT implies panpsychism: any system with non-zero Φ is conscious to some degree, including thermostats or photodiodes (though minimally).
  • IIT provides a mathematical framework for quantifying consciousness, enabling empirical testing.

Criticisms:

  • Φ may be computationally intractable to calculate for large systems.
  • The theory’s panpsychist implications are counterintuitive.
  • The 2025 adversarial test did not decisively support IIT. \[sciencedaily.com\]

3.2.2 6.2 Global Neuronal Workspace Theory (GNWT)

Global Neuronal Workspace Theory, developed by Bernard Baars and Stanislas Dehaene, proposes that consciousness arises when information is broadcast widely across the brain.

Core claims:

  • Unconscious processes are modular and encapsulated.
  • Consciousness occurs when information is selected and broadcast to a “global workspace” of widely connected neurons.
  • This broadcast makes information available to multiple cognitive systems (attention, memory, executive control).

Implications:

  • Consciousness is a threshold phenomenon: information becomes conscious when it ignites widespread activity.
  • Consciousness is closely linked to reportability and access.

Criticisms:

  • GNWT explains access consciousness but may not explain phenomenal consciousness.
  • The 2025 findings de-emphasized prefrontal areas central to GNWT. \[sciencedaily.com\]

3.2.3 6.3 Higher-Order Theories (HOT)

Higher-Order Theories propose that a mental state is conscious when it is the object of a higher-order representation (a thought about the state, or a perception of the state).

Variants:

  • Higher-Order Thought (HOT): Consciousness requires a meta-cognitive thought about a mental state.
  • Higher-Order Perception (HOP): Consciousness requires a perceptual-like monitoring of a mental state.

Implications:

  • Consciousness involves reflexivity or self-awareness.
  • Simple organisms lacking higher-order capacities are not conscious.

Criticisms:

  • Circularity: What makes the higher-order state itself conscious?
  • Introspection suggests many conscious states lack explicit meta-awareness.

3.2.4 6.4 Enactivism and Embodied Consciousness

Enactivism, rooted in phenomenology and biology, holds that consciousness arises through embodied interaction with the environment.

Core claims:

  • Consciousness is not a passive representation but an active enaction of a world.
  • Life and mind are continuous: living systems are inherently cognitive.
  • Consciousness depends on sensorimotor coupling and autonomy.

Implications:

  • Even simple organisms are conscious if they enact their world through autonomous sensorimotor activity.
  • Consciousness is relational, not localized in brains.

Criticisms:

  • Enactivism is conceptually rich but lacks quantitative, testable predictions.
  • The notion of “enacting a world” is metaphorically evocative but scientifically vague.

3.2.5 6.5 Panpsychism and Russellian Monism

Panpsychism holds that consciousness (or proto-consciousness) is a fundamental feature of reality, present in all physical entities.

Variants:

  • Constitutive panpsychism: Macro-level consciousness is constituted by micro-level consciousness.
  • Emergent panpsychism: Consciousness emerges from proto-conscious elements combining in certain ways.

Russellian monism (also called neutral monism or dual-aspect monism) proposes that physical and mental properties are two aspects of an underlying neutral substrate. Bertrand Russell argued that physics describes the extrinsic, relational structure of matter but not its intrinsic nature—which may be proto-experiential.

Implications:

  • Panpsychism avoids the hard problem by making consciousness fundamental, not emergent.
  • It faces the combination problem: how do micro-experiences combine into unified macro-experience?

Criticisms:

  • Panpsychism seems to multiply mysteries rather than solve them.
  • The combination problem is arguably as hard as the hard problem.

3.2.6 6.6 Quantum Theories: Penrose-Hameroff Orch-OR

Orchestrated Objective Reduction (Orch-OR), proposed by Roger Penrose and Stuart Hameroff, suggests that consciousness arises from quantum processes in neuronal microtubules.

Core claims:

  • Quantum coherence occurs in microtubules (protein structures inside neurons).
  • Objective reduction (collapse of the quantum wavefunction) produces moments of conscious experience.
  • Consciousness is a fundamental feature of the universe, linked to quantum gravity.

Implications:

  • Consciousness is non-computable and irreducible to classical computation.
  • Proto-consciousness exists at the quantum level.

Criticisms:

  • Quantum coherence in warm, wet brains is unlikely (decoherence is too rapid).
  • Experimental evidence is disputed.
  • Even if quantum processes occur, it’s unclear why they would be conscious.

3.2.7 Summary

Contemporary theories of consciousness range from reductionist (GNWT) to non-reductionist (IIT, enactivism, panpsychism, Orch-OR). Each captures different aspects: IIT emphasizes integration, GNWT emphasizes access, enactivism emphasizes embodiment, panpsychism emphasizes fundamentality, Orch-OR emphasizes quantum foundations. No single theory is universally accepted, and the 2025 adversarial test underscored the difficulty of decisive empirical adjudication.

3.2.8 Open Question

If multiple theories of consciousness partially capture the truth, does this suggest that consciousness is a multidimensional phenomenon requiring a pluralistic framework rather than a single unified theory?
